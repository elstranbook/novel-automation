My morning had that rare, slow drip of quiet, the kind where time seemed to stretch lazily between the cawing of crows and the distant rumble of a lawnmower grinding somewhere down the street. “Seriously, who even likes kale? It’s just crunchy green regret,” I fired back, chuckling despite the dull nag of something beneath my smirk.

Maya’s reply came before I could blink: “Crunchy green regret? Only you could make salad sound existential. Next you’ll say water has an attitude problem.”

I swiped my hair back and typed, “Pretty sure water’s judging me for that third slice of pizza last night.”

The banter was a lifeline, a familiar rhythm—our way of occupying the spaces where real feelings hovered too close for comfort. I savored the flurry, even as my fingertips hesitated over a snarky retort to some viral post Maya had dragged into our chat. Despite the easy laughs, an itch of unease curled in my stomach. Could it be the quiet underneath the noise, that prickling sensation as I searched for something—I wasn’t sure what—in the digital chatter? For now, sarcasm was my armor, the mask I could slip on without thinking, keeping deeper questions at bay.



Midway through the morning scroll, a notification blinked up from my phone, different somehow. Not Maya, not another meme from the group chat. Liam.

His name sat there, like a quiet echo behind my thumb’s idle, the message unopened.

I hesitated, then tapped the screen, fingers trembling just slightly. The text was a cryptic fragment. “We need to talk.” No emojis. No easy context.

My heart thumped a little too loudly in the empty room. I hovered over the reply box, thumb poised but frozen. Should I answer now? Wait? What did that mean exactly?

I swiped up to check the read receipts, fingers slick with a nervous sweat. But Liam’s message remained in a ghostly unread state, his own blue ticks stubbornly absent.

That contrast hit hard—Maya’s nonstop barrage of chaos on one hand, and this quiet bubble of silence from Liam on the other. And suddenly, my sarcastic veil felt threadbare. The laughter and barbs dissolved into an ache of longing and confusion, tangled with an icy spike of anxiety. Was this silence a wall or a crack? I stared at the phone as if it might answer for him, but the screen only flickered cold, offering no comfort.



Later, I curled into the corner of my room, pulling my sketchbook toward me not for drawing but for words. The room felt heavier now, the shadows longer, like the unease that had bloomed since that unread message.

Pen scraping against paper, I let myself spill the raw, skeptical thoughts I usually bottled up: texts left unopened, conversations petering out, the thinning threads of connection that felt harder to grasp each day. No witty filter, no sharp comeback to deflect the ache—just the bare, uneasy truth whispering between the lines.

Writing was like pulling the armor off inch by inch. The words weren’t neat or finished; they stumbled awkwardly, honest and raw. I traced over ‘Lonely?’ before crossing it out, erasing and rewriting the question marks, because admitting it felt like stepping off a ledge.

The room smelled faintly of old paper and lavender from the candle I'd lit earlier, but no scent cut through the quiet tension that pressed on my chest. This wasn’t the sarcasm shield I knew how to wield—this was vulnerability bleeding through the cracks. I questioned if confronting this truth would make me stronger or just leave me exposed in the cold.

My pencil paused, hovering. The page in front of me was no longer a safe place—it was a mirror. And right now, I wasn’t sure what I wanted to see.

"In a world that’s constantly messaging and scrolling," she said, eyes scanning our half-attentive faces, "what does it really mean to be honest? To be vulnerable?"

Her voice was warm, inviting. She pushed us to think about what we said—and what we left unsaid. The air buzzed with a hesitant energy as classmates traded glances, some eager, others reluctant. I folded my arms, trying to keep my skepticism close, but her words unsettled the usual calm behind my eyes.

As the discussion unfolded, I wrestled internally. This was more than an assignment; it was a challenge—a chance, maybe, to face the silences I’d been avoiding, the ones that lurked in every unread message and half-dropped conversation.

The bell rang, echoing in the mix of heartbeats and thoughts, and I tucked the idea away, unsure if I was ready to open that door.



By the time evening rolled around, the house was quiet except for the soft hum of the refrigerator and the occasional creak of settling wood. I sat cross-legged on the couch, phone in hand, scrolling again out of habit rather than hope.
